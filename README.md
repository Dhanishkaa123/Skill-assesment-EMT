## 5. Real-Time Applications in Chandrayaan-3

### a) Lander Electrostatic Discharge (ESD) Protection
During descent, the **Vikram** lander faced electrostatic charging caused by friction between landing jets and lunar dust. Electric field intensity sensors monitored the local field, allowing discharge circuits to neutralize excess charge and prevent electronic damage.

### b) Plasma and Thruster Control
Ionized gases expelled from the propulsion system generated localized electric fields. Engineers calculated field intensity to ensure stable thrust direction and maintain the delicate balance required for soft-landing in low-gravity conditions.

### c) Surface Dust and Electric Field Studies
The **Pragyan** rover‚Äôs payload included sensors to detect the Moon‚Äôs electric environment. Sunlight and solar wind create charged dust layers near the surface, forming electric fields that cause dust levitation. Understanding these fields helps in designing future lunar habitats and equipment that resist dust accumulation.

### d) Antenna Signal Transmission
Radio antennas aboard the lander rely on uniform electric fields for consistent signal propagation. The field intensity around antenna elements determines the strength and direction of transmitted data ‚Äî crucial for maintaining contact between Earth and the lander.

## 6. Electric Field Lines and Equipotential Surfaces
Electric field lines represent the direction of force, while equipotential surfaces are regions where potential remains constant. Chandrayaan-3‚Äôs sensors were designed to maintain equipotential surfaces across sensitive circuits, ensuring consistent electric field distribution and reducing noise.

## 7. Electric Field in Spacecraft Materials
The lunar environment lacks an atmosphere, leading to charging effects due to solar radiation and particle impacts. ISRO used composite materials with controlled dielectric constants to minimize charge buildup and maintain safe electric field intensity levels.
